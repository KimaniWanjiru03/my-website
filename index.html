About Me

Hello! I’m Immaculate Wanjiru Kimani, a Data Scientist and Consultant specializing in financial engineering, machine learning, and impact evaluation. I have a strong background in data science, actuarial science, and financial modeling, and I use these skills to drive insights in financial inclusion, risk assessment, and education initiatives.

Currently, I work as a Consultant – Data Scientist & Math Olympiad Manager for the AHEEN RDP Pathways Program, where I lead data-driven program evaluations and support STEM education for displaced learners. I also serve as a Project Manager at Lizzette Agencies, overseeing research projects that inform business strategy through high-quality data collection and analysis.

My journey in data science and financial analysis started with a BSc in Actuarial Science, followed by an MSc in Mathematical Sciences (Data Science Major) from AIMS Rwanda. I am currently expanding my expertise in MSc. Financial Engineering at WorldQuant University.

Over the years, I’ve worked on diverse projects, including credit risk assessment for Kenyan banks, financial influencer analytics using AI, and education data management for refugee programs. My work with organizations like UNHCR, ESOMAR, and Genesis Analytics has strengthened my expertise in data-driven decision-making, monitoring & evaluation (MEAL), and stakeholder engagement.

Beyond my professional roles, I am passionate about women in STEM, financial inclusion, and the ethical use of AI. I actively participate in international research conferences and have volunteered at events like the ESOMAR Congress in Athens and ICII in Accra.

When I’m not working, you’ll find me hiking, experimenting with new recipes, or refining my skills in Python, R, and Julia. I believe in using technology and data to create a more inclusive, informed, and impactful world.

Projects

Leveraging Artificial Intelligence for Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning (MEL) in Education in Emergencies: A Case Study of the AHEEN RDP Pathways Program

Ongoing Research — 2025

This study examines how artificial intelligence (AI) can transform monitoring, evaluation, and learning (MEL) systems for refugee education programs, using the AHEEN RDP Pathways Program as a case study.

Unveiling the Influence: Exploring Financial Inclusion through Social Media Influencers in Emerging Economies

Ongoing Research — 2024

This ongoing project investigates the role of social media influencers, or "Finfluencers," in promoting financial services in emerging economies. Using advanced AI and machine learning techniques, the project explores how influencers can bridge barriers to financial inclusion and improve financial literacy.

Benefits of Inclusive Insurance: A Case Study of the Low-Income Population in Kenya

MSc. Thesis — 2023

This project examines the impact of inclusive insurance on Kenya's low-income population, with a focus on changes in capital as a benefit measure. The study utilized an absorbing Markov chain analysis and machine learning models to predict insurance status based on demographic data. The Random Forest model yielded the most accurate predictions, identifying key variables such as education level, motive, income range, and dependents as important factors in determining insurance status.

This research provides valuable insights into the efficacy of inclusive insurance and sheds light on the factors influencing insurance coverage among underserved populations.

Rarita National Team Selection and Analysis

Research Project — 2022

This study, commissioned by the Society of Actuaries, aimed to create a competitive national team for Rarita that would have both economic benefits and international ranking potential. Actuarial methods were extensively used to develop metrics and strategies for team selection and performance improvement.

Credit Risk Assessment of Personal Loans Using Non-Parametric Survival Models

BSc. Thesis — 2021

This study explored the application of survival analysis to credit risk modeling for banks in Kenya. Using non-parametric models like Kaplan-Meier and Nelson-Aalen, the research aimed to estimate the probability of default and understand the impact of censored data on credit scoring. The findings emphasized the need to account for macroeconomic factors in credit risk assessments, providing valuable insights into the field of credit risk management.
